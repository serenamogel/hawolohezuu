About Pope Francis

Bergoglio, who was born in Buenos Aires, Argentina, started his career as a technician in a food science lab and afterwards worked as a bouncer and cleaner. He was motivated to join the Jesuits in 1958 after he recovered from a serious illness. In 1969 he was ordained as a Catholic priest, and from 1973 to 1979 he led the Jesuits in Argentina as provincial superior. In 1998, he was appointed Archbishop of Buenos Aires, and in 2001, Pope John Paul II elevated him to the rank of cardinal.

During the riots that took place in Argentina in December of 2001, he was at the helm of the local Church. He was seen as a threat by both Néstor and Cristina Fernández de Kirchner’s administrations. Bergoglio was chosen by a papal conclave to succeed Pope Benedict XVI on 13 March 2013, following Benedict’s retirement on 28 February 2013. In honour of Saint Francis of Assisi, he decided to take the name Francis as his papal name.

Francis has been a public figure who has been recognised for his lowliness, his focus on God’s mercy, his global prominence as pope, his care for the poor, and his dedication to interfaith understanding. He is said to have taken a more relaxed attitude to the papacy than his predecessors by, among other things, eschewing the papal apartments in the Apostolic Palace in favour of the more humble quarters at the Domus Sanctae Marthae.
